Introduction

The AnAge Database of Animal Ageing and Longevity is a curated database of ageing and life history in animals that was primarily developed for comparative biology studies. It contains information, compiled from hundreds of scientific papers, for over 4,200 species. Its mainly meant for studies of aging, so for each species, it includes variables such as lifespan, age of sexual maturity, adult body mass, typical body temperature, and metabolic rate. We will study this data to learn more about the relationships between each variable and the key outcome variable: lifespan.

The goal of the study is to determine how the metabolic rate of animals of the Chordata phylum may be related to lifespan. The data comes from the AnAge Database of Animal Ageing and Longevity, which curates information from research papers of more than four thousand animal species with variables related to ageing. The dataset we are working with contains 347 data points of different Chordata animals from this database. We wish to determine if 1) a lower metabolic rate increases lifespan, 2) if the relationship between the two variables is nonlinear, and 3) the estimated lifespan of an animal with the characteristics of a crab-eating racoon with a 50% smaller metabolic rate.

The analysis concluded that the relationship between Metabolic Rate by Mass and Longevity was best modeled by a linear relationship between the logarithm of the two variables. Animals with lower metabolic rates were found to have a longer lifespan on average. The predicted increase in lifespan in an animal with the metabolic rate of a crab-eating raccoon reduced by 50% was approximately between 32.26% and 32.98%.

Exploratory Data Analysis

For the purposes of this study, we are specifically interested in animals metabolism rates (rate of energy use in Watts) and their maximum lifespan in years. These variables will be referred to as Metabolic Rate and Maximum Longevity. It is of note that animals of greater mass will require a greater amount of energy and thus a higher metabolic rate. In order to be able to compare animals of different sizes, we define a new variable of Metabolic Rate by Mass, which is an animals Metabolic Rate divided by its body mass in grams (this new variable simply uses the units of Watts per gram). This is the variable we will use for measuring metabolism for the rest of the report.

Modeling & Diagnostics

We first construct a linear model of the form

\[\text{ln(Longevity)} \sim \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{ln(MetabolicRate)}\] The resulting model has a \(\beta_0 = 0.1492\) and \(\beta_1 = -0.3999\), the latter with a p-value of 2.2e-16, so we can conclude it is significant.

Out of a desire to try fitting a more complex model, we also construct a natural cubic spline of made up of piecewise polynomials with the number of knots depending on its degrees of freedom (df). To find the 95% confidence interval of our predicted value, we use a pivotal confidence interval by performing 1000 bootstrap iterations. Results

By viewing Figure 6, we can see that the association between metabolic rate and lifespan is negative: lower metabolic rates do indeed have longer lifespans.

We wish to predict the longevity of a crab-eating raccoon (HAGRID = 1898) with a 50% reduced metabolic rate using this model. A crab-eating raccoons metabolic rate by mass is about 0.0022, and its 50% reduced metabolic rater by mass is about 0.0011. With our model, we find that the predicted lifespan for this metabolic rate is about 2.8681 ln(years), or about 17.6 years. This prediction is lower than the actual maximum lifespan of a crab-eating raccoon, 19 years, but greater than the models predicted lifespan for an animal with the unmodified metabolism of the raccoon, 13.3 years. (Note that the models predicted lifespan being less than the actual lifespan occurs with the spline model as well, with the spline model predicting 18.2 years, so this is not necessarily indicative of model performance.) We can instead say that the model yielded a 32.33% increase in predicted lifespan on average for an animal with the lifespan of these characteristics.

We use the 1000 bootstrap samples to obtain that many estimates of the the predicted value and first perform a Shapiro-Wilk test to test the null hypothesis that the bootstrap estimates are normal, which is an assumption of the t-test, which we use to find the 95% confidence interval. The Shapiro-Wilk test resulted in a p-value of 0.5536, which is large enough to not reject that the estimate are normally distributed. The t-test of comparing the mean of the bootstrap samples to the linear models predicted value yielded a p-value of 0.1588, meaning we do not reject the null hypothesis that the true mean (of the bootstrap samples of the estimate) is equal to the models prediction. The 95% confidence interval obtained from the t-test is \([2.86733, 2.872804]\) ln(years), which is equal to an interval of \([17.58999, 17.68654]\) years or a percent increase of \([32.25557\%, 32.98152\%]\) in lifespan from a metabolic rate reduction of 50%.

Conclusions

This study ultimately determined that the relationship between metabolic rate and lifespan was that the two were negatively associated, so a lower metabolic rate results in a higher lifespan. Between a linear model and a spline model, we concluded that the variables would be best modeled with a log-transformed linear regression model. Using this model, we found that an animal with the metabolic rate of a crab-eating raccoon reduced by 50% would have an estimated lifespan increase of between 32.26% and 32.98% on average.

Limitations to this analysis is that since both the response and predictor variable were log-transformed, the naive back-transformation of the variables to compute the confidence interval may not have been appropriate. Additionally, the data of about 300 animals contained a number of non-mammals (32 of them, to be exact), and a more accurate model for estimating the lifespan for mammalian humans could be found by using a larger dataset of mammals exclusively. Fitting a multivariate regression may also have yielded a more sophisticated model.
